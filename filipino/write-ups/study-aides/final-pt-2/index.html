There are three marker types: ang markers, ng markers, and sa markers. The marker that should be used mainly depends on three things:

  • Singular vs Plural
  • Common vs Proper
  • Usage

First, here's the actual markers:

Marker Table
Ang Markers Ng Markers Sa Markers
Singular Plural Singular Plural Singular Plural
Common ang ang mga ng ng mga sa sa mga
Proper si sina ni nina kay kina

It's not enough to know the words themselves though, you need to also know the appropriate usages for each marker.

Note: While the below information is correct and should get you the correct answer on the final (at least most of the time), the way it is presented might not be technically proper. Technically, ang markers are ALWAYS denote the subject; it's just that what constitutes the subject changes based on the verb affix being used. For example, um- verbs are actor focused verbs: Therefore, in sentences with um verbs, the actor/doer is the subject of the sentence and is marked with an ang marker. Contrast this with -in verbs, which are goal/object-focused verbs. This means the subject of the sentence is the goal/object - NOT the doer - and therefore, the goal/object of the sentence receives the ang marker - NOT the doer. If you're confused by this (and if you trust me), don't worry too much. The usages presented below simply take the necessary logical conclusions from the rules above and lumps them together for ease of learning. I'm mostly just putting this note here because I don't like leaving information out.

Common Usages of Ang Markers

  • Denoting Subject of Sentence
  • Denoting Doer of Um / Mag / Ma / Mang / Man / Mam Verbs
    • Common Structure: Verb + Ang Marker + Doer(s)
    • Examples:
      1. Tumakbo ang bata. (The child ran.)
      2. Mag-aawit ang mga estudyante (The students will study.)
      3. Natutulog si Felice. (Felice is sleeping).
      4. Nangingisda sina Rod at Earnest. (Rod and Earnest are fishing.)
  • Denoting Goal/Recipient of In/Hin / An/Han / I Verbs
    • Common Structure: Verb + Ng Marker + Doer + Ang Marker + Direct Object
    • Examples:
      1. Niluto ng kusinero ang adobo (The chef cooked the adobo.)
      2. Lulutuhan ni Gabe sina Ned at Maria . (Gabe will cook for Ned and Maria.)
  • Identificational Sentences
    • Identificational sentences identify one person/object as another.
    • Common Structure: Ang Marker + Noun/Adjective + (ay) + Ang Marker + Noun/Adjective
    • Examples:
      1. Si Trump ang pangulo. (Trump is the president.)
      2. Si Precy ay ang guro. (Precy is the teacher.)
      3. Ang diyanitor ang musikero (The janitor is the musician.)
      4. Sina Lonzo at Brandon ang mga manlalaro (Lonzo and Brandon are the players).

Tips for Ang Markers

Basically, every sentence should have an ang marker/pronoun/demonstrative, so if you don't see one, really consider using one. Also, any independent clause (a part of a sentence that can stand by itself) should only have one ang marker. So if you do see an ang marker in a given clause. strongly consider using another marker type. Identificational sentences (see above) are an exception. An example of a sentences with parallel/independent clauses (and consequently two ang markers): Tumakbo si Lois at kumain si Jaryd. (Lois ran and Jaryd ate.)

Common Usages of Ng Markers

  • Denoting Doer of In/Hin / An/Han / I Verbs
    • Common Structure: Verb + Ng Marker + Doer + Ang Marker + Direct Object
    • Examples:
      1. Niluto ng kusinero ang adobo (The chef cooked the adobo.)
  • Denoting Direct Object of Um / Mag / Ma / Mang / Man / Mam Verbs
    • Common Structure: Verb + Ang Marker + Doer + Ng Marker + Direct Object/Recipient
    • : Examples:
      1. Kumakain si Josh ng adobo. (Josh is eating the adobo.)
      2. Maglilinis ang mga bata ng mga kotse. (The children will clean the cars).
  • Denoting Posession:
    • Common Structure: Object Being Owned + Ng Marker + Owner
    • Examples:
      1. kotses ni Jason (Jason's car)
      2. klase nina Bob at Joe (Bob and Joe's class)

Tips for Ng Marker

The doer of of in/hin, an/han, and i verbs is implied sometimes, so you do not always need to mark the doer. You do ALWAYS need an ang marker/pronoun/demonstrative. So if you see one of the verb types mentioned above, check first for an ang marker. If there's not one, you should consider that. After that, consider using a ng marker to denote the doer. Always consider the context of the sentence.

Common Usages of Sa Markers

  • Denoting Ownership
    • Common Structure: Sa- Marker + Owner + Noun
      • Ex: Sa mga estudyante ang mga libro iyon. (Those are the students' books.)
  • Denoting Recipient/Purpose/Location of Verb
    • Common Structure: Verb + Denoted Doer (or Recip/Purp/Loc) + (para) Denoted Recip/Purp/Loc (or Doer)
    • Ex: Si Vivien ay gumawa ng keyk para kay Joseph. (Vivien made the cake for Joseph.)

Note: I'm a little fuzzy on when you use sa markers vs ng markers vs ang markers to denote recipients. If I had to take a guess, for most of the verbs associated with ang doers (um, mag, ma, etc.), the sa marker is used most often. With the verbs with ng doers, the ang marker usually denotes the recipient. It really depends on verb type and context though. I'm also not super sure about why there's two ang pronouns/markers/demonstratives in the sentence 'Sa mga estudyante ang mga libro iyon'. I think it has to do with some weird identificational stuff (identifying 'iyon' as 'ang mga libro'), but I have no idea.

Tips for Sa Markers

The biggest hint that a sa marker should be used is the presence of the word 'para' before the blank space. It's probably a sa marker in that case. Also, a blank space at the beginning of the sentence is likely either a ang or a sa marker.

TL;DR

  • Filipino sentences look weird without an ang marker/pronoun/demonstrative, so if there's not one, really consider using one.
  • There's usually only one ang marker, except for parallel/indepent clauses and identificational statemetns.
  • If you see an in/hin, an/han, or i verb - and there is already an ang marker, consider using a ng marker to denote the doer of the verb.
  • Blank spaces after the word 'para' are likely filled with a sa marker.
  • Blank spaces at the beginning of sentences are likely filled with a sa marker or an ang marker.
  • Seriously, always look for the presence (or lack thereof) of ang markers/pronouns/demonstratives.
