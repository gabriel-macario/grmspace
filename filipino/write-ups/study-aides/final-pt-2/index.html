Close 'Introduction'

There are three marker types: ang markers, ng markers, and sa markers. The marker that should be used mainly depends on three things:

  • Singular vs Plural
  • Common vs Proper
  • Usage

First, here's the actual markers:

Marker Table
Ang Markers Ng Markers Sa Markers
Singular Plural Singular Plural Singular Plural
Common ang ang mga ng ng mga sa sa mga
Proper si sina ni nina kay kina

It's not enough to know the words themselves though, you need to also know the appropriate usages for each marker.

Note: While the below information is correct and should get you the correct answer on the final (at least most of the time), the way it is presented might not be technically proper. Technically, ang markers are ALWAYS denote the subject; it's just that what constitutes the subject changes based on the verb affix being used. For example, um- verbs are actor focused verbs: Therefore, in sentences with um verbs, the actor/doer is the subject of the sentence and is marked with an ang marker. Contrast this with -in verbs, which are goal/object-focused verbs. This means the subject of the sentence is the goal/object - NOT the doer - and therefore, the goal/object of the sentence receives the ang marker - NOT the doer. If you're confused by this (and if you trust me), don't worry too much. The usages presented below simply take the necessary logical conclusions from the rules above and lumps them together for ease of learning. I'm mostly just putting this note here because I don't like leaving information out.

Common Usages of Ang Markers

  • Denoting Subject of Sentence
  • Denoting Doer of Um / Mag / Ma / Mang / Man / Mam Verbs
    • Common Structure: Verb + Ang Marker + Doer(s)
    • Examples:
      1. Tumakbo ang bata. (The child ran.)
      2. Mag-aawit ang mga estudyante (The students will study.)
      3. Natutulog si Felice. (Felice is sleeping).
      4. Nangingisda sina Rod at Earnest. (Rod and Earnest are fishing.)
  • Denoting Goal/Recipient of In/Hin / An/Han / I Verbs
    • Common Structure: Verb + Ng Marker + Doer + Ang Marker + Direct Object
    • Examples:
      1. Niluto ng kusinero ang adobo (The chef cooked the adobo.)
      2. Lulutuhan ni Gabe sina Ned at Maria . (Gabe will cook for Ned and Maria.)
  • Identificational Sentences
    • Identificational sentences identify one person/object as another.
    • Common Structure: Ang Marker + Noun/Adjective + (ay) + Ang Marker + Noun/Adjective
    • Examples:
      1. Si Trump ang pangulo. (Trump is the president.)
      2. Si Precy ay ang guro. (Precy is the teacher.)
      3. Ang diyanitor ang musikero (The janitor is the musician.)
      4. Sina Lonzo at Brandon ang mga manlalaro (Lonzo and Brandon are the players).

Tips for Ang Markers

Basically, every sentence should have an ang marker/pronoun/demonstrative, so if you don't see one, really consider using one. Also, any independent clause (a part of a sentence that can stand by itself) should only have one ang marker. So if you do see an ang marker in a given clause. strongly consider using another marker type. Identificational sentences (see above) are an exception. An example of a sentences with parallel/independent clauses (and consequently two ang markers): Tumakbo si Lois at kumain si Jaryd. (Lois ran and Jaryd ate.)

.

Common Usages of Ng Markers

  • Denoting Doer of In/Hin / An/Han / I Verbs
    • Common Structure: Verb + Ng Marker + Doer + Ang Marker + Direct Object
    • Examples:
      1. Niluto ng kusinero ang adobo (The chef cooked the adobo.)
  • Denoting Direct Object of Um / Mag / Ma / Mang / Man / Mam Verbs
    • Common Structure: Verb + Ang Marker + Doer + Ng Marker + Direct Object/Recipient
    • : Examples:
      1. Kumakain si Josh ng adobo. (Josh is eating the adobo.)
      2. Maglilinis ang mga bata ng mga kotse. (The children will clean the cars).
  • Denoting Posession:
    • Common Structure: Object Being Owned + Ng Marker + Owner
    • Examples:
      1. kotses ni Jason (Jason's car)
      2. klase nina Bob at Joe (Bob and Joe's class)

Tips for Ng Marker

The doer of of in/hin, an/han, and i verbs is implied sometimes, so you do not always need to mark the doer. You do ALWAYS need an ang marker/pronoun/demonstrative. So if you see one of the verb types mentioned above, check first for an ang marker. If there's not one, you should consider that. After that, consider using a ng marker to denote the doer. Always consider the context of the sentence.

Common Usages of Sa Markers

  • Denoting Ownership
    • Common Structure: Sa- Marker + Owner + Noun
      • Ex: Sa mga estudyante ang mga libro iyon. (Those are the students' books.)
  • Denoting Recipient/Purpose/Location of Verb
    • Common Structure: Verb + Denoted Doer (or Recip/Purp/Loc) + (para) Denoted Recip/Purp/Loc (or Doer)
    • Ex: Si Vivien ay gumawa ng keyk para kay Joseph. (Vivien made the cake for Joseph.)

Note: I'm a little fuzzy on when you use sa markers vs ng markers vs ang markers to denote recipients. If I had to take a guess, for most of the verbs associated with ang doers (um, mag, ma, etc.), the sa marker is used most often. With the verbs with ng doers, the ang marker usually denotes the recipient. It really depends on verb type and context though. I'm also not super sure about why there's two ang pronouns/markers/demonstratives in the sentence 'Sa mga estudyante ang mga libro iyon'. I think it has to do with some weird identificational stuff (identifying 'iyon' as 'ang mga libro'), but I have no idea.

Tips for Sa Markers

The biggest hint that a sa marker should be used is the presence of the word 'para' before the blank space. It's probably a sa marker in that case. Also, a blank space at the beginning of the sentence is likely either a ang or a sa marker.

TL;DR

  • Filipino sentences look weird without an ang marker/pronoun/demonstrative, so if there's not one, really consider using one.
  • There's usually only one ang marker, except for parallel/indepent clauses and identificational statemetns.
  • If you see an in/hin, an/han, or i verb - and there is already an ang marker, consider using a ng marker to denote the doer of the verb.
  • Blank spaces after the word 'para' are likely filled with a sa marker.
  • Blank spaces at the beginning of sentences are likely filled with a sa marker or an ang marker.
  • Seriously, always look for the presence (or lack thereof) of ang markers/pronouns/demonstratives.

Every type of marker (ang/ng/sa) has their own set of pronouns that can take the place of any instance of: Marker + Noun(s) Being Marked.

tayo (inclusive)
Second Person ikaw / ka* kayo
Third Person siya sila

* - Ikaw is used at the beginning and end of sentences (e.g. "Ikaw ay kumain." / "Ang guro ay ikaw." ). I think the only exceptions are super simple sentences like "Kumain ka."

Singular Plural

natin (inclusive)

Sa Pronouns
Singular Plural
First Person akin amin (exclusive)

Second Person iyo inyo
Third Person kaniya kanila

Note: You will often see sa pronouns linked to a noun being posessed via the 'ng' linker (e.g. "kaniyang bola" / "aking asawa").

Alongside pronouns, there are also demonstratives which can take the place of any instance of: Marker + Noun(s) being marked (though I expect they are more commonly used with common objects, rather than people).

These are roughly equivalent to the English words/phrases 'this', 'that', and 'that over there'. There is a demonstrative for when the object being spoken of is closer to the speaker than the listener, when it is equally close to the speaker and listener, and when it is far (yonder) from both. And just like with pronouns, each type of marker has it's own set of demonstratives to alongside it.

Demonstratives
Closer to Speaker (this) Equally Close (that) Far Off (yonder/there)
Ang Demonstratives ito iyan iyon
Ng Demonstratives nito niyan niyon
Sa Demonstratives dito diyan doon

Sina Dana at Anjelica ay maglalaro. Maglalaro _______.
And then you'll have to fill in the blank with the appropriate pronoun. In this example, 'Sina' is an ang marker and 'Dana at Anjelica' are the nouns being marked. So you replace the marker + noun(s) being marked with the appropriate pronoun: In this case, the ang pronoun for plural, third person: 'sila''

If it's not formatted like the above, then it'll just be like the marker section. The usages for each type of pronoun are the same for their respective marker. So the doer um verbs can be represented by both an ang marker + marked noun OR an ang pronoun. Doer of in verbs can be denoted by the ng marker or be represented by an ng pronoun. Etcetera, etcetera.

Tips

There's a few giveaways of which word to use. A pronoun after the space, usually with an -ng linker, in answered with one of 'mayroon/meron/wala.' A 'mga' after the space is answered with 'may. If you see an ‘-ng’ linkers that wouldn’t normally be there (so ignore adjectives or nouns that are linked to adjectives), then that’s probably a sign for ‘mayroon’ or ‘wala’ also.'

If (that's an italicized, bolded and underlined if), she gives you choices on a question by question basis for this section, and if you believe that Tita would only give one correct answer per problem, then you can sometimes get to the answer by choosing the non-redundant answer.

For example, if you're given 'may', 'mayroon', and 'wala', the answer is probably going to be 'may', since both 'mayroon' and 'wala' basically follow the same rules of usage gramatically speaking.

For another example, if you're given 'may', 'mayroong', and 'meron', it's likely 'meron' because in most situations, 'may' and 'mayroong' are interchangeable.

If you understood what I meant above by arriving at the answer via redundancy, here's a quick cheat sheet of what's redundant with what:

  1. Mayroon ~ Wala ~ Meron
  2. Mayroong ~ Merong ~ Walang ~ May

So if you have three choices, and two of them are in the same number above (e.g. 'mayroong' & 'walang'), then take a good look at the third choice. If they're all redundant, double check to make sure that none of the giveaways apply ('mga' / pronouns with -ng linkers). If their all redundant AND there's no dead giveaway... ¯\_(ツ)_/¯

Close '3. May/Mayroon/Wala'

Won't lie, don't really know what she wants us to do here, but choosing what linker to use is pretty simple, and is based on the last letter of the word before the linker.

If the last letter of the word before the linker ends in a...

  • Vowel: Add an -ng to the end of the word
    • Ex: magandang babae (beautiful woman)
  • Non-'n' Consonant: Put the linker word 'na' after the word
    • Ex: mabait na bata (nice/kind child)
  • The letter 'n': Add a '-g' at the end of the word
    • Ex: mayamang tao (rich person)

Nouns and adjectives can be linked in any order, and if there are multiple adjectives connected to the same noun, they must all be linked.

  • mayamang magandang babae (rich beautiful woman)
  • mayamang babaeng maganda (rich beautiful woman)
  • babaeng mabait na maganda (nice/kind beautiful woman)

Note: This section is just a slightly edited version of the verbs section in my midterm study aide. (I'm lazy)

This is the most important thing I can tell you: Because she'll likely give us an example conjugation of the verb, you don't need to worry about any of the wonky stuff: Assimilation, glottal stops, dashes, irregular verbs, etc. There is only one exception to this, and that's with in/hin- verbs. But before that, we'll start with conjugation rules for all the other verb forms.

Note: There are many ways to write out the rules for conjugations; For test-taking purposes, the ones listed below are structure such that given any single conjugation, you can arrive at any other conjugation. If you have already established your own rule system that gives you the correct answers, don't worry about what I've written below. Do what works for you.

-Um- Verb Rules
Rule Example
Root N/A takbo
Infinitive um before first vowel of root tumakbo
Past same as infinitive tumakbo
Present first two syllables of infinitive + root tumatakbo
Future first syllable of root + root tatakbo

Note: Um- verbs conjugate the same even if root word starts with a vowel: 'alis' -> 'umalis/umalis/umaalis/aalis'

.
Ma- Verb Rules
Rule Example
Root N/A ligo
Infinitive ma + root maligo
Past turn first m of infinitive to n naligo
Present past with repeated second syllable naliligo
Future turn first n of present to m maliligo

Ma- verbs conjugate the same even if root word starts with a vowel. In the appropriate chapter of the text, there is a dash, but the instructor told us not to put that dash. Additionally, the ma- verb table in the text's appendices does not have a dash: 'uhaw' -> 'mauhaw/nauhaw/nauuhaw/mauuhaw'.

Also, this m/n/n/m pattern (with the repeating syllable for the present and future conjugations), is mirrored across every verb prefix that starts with an 'm' (at least that we've learned in class): ma-/mag-/mang-/man-/mam-. Take a look:

Mag- Verb Rules (General)
Rule Example
Root N/A linis
Infinitive mag + root maglinis
Past turn first m of infinitive to n naglinis
Present past with repeated second syllable naglilinis
Future turn first n of present to m maglilinis

If the root word starts with a vowel, you put a dash after the prefix: 'iwan' -> 'mag-iwan/nag-iwan/nag-iiwan/mag-iiwan. But, assuming an example is provided on the test, it should be obvious whether or not a dash is needed.

Mang/Man/Mam- Verb Rules
Rule Example
Root N/A gamot
Infinitive mang/man/mam + (assimilated) root manggamot
Past turn first m of infinitive to n nanggamot
Present past with repeated second syllable nanggagamot
Future turn first n of present to m manggagamot

Note: Sometimes, when the root word starts with a vowel, mang- verbs will have a dash after the prefix. Again, whether or not a dash should be included will be evident if the instructor gives example conjugations.

These three prefixes are lumped together because these are the only 'm' prefixes where assimilation occurs. But if you don't know what that is, or when it happens, or whatever, you don't have to worry about it. The assimilation, if there is any, will have already occurred in any example she gives, so you just need to apply/reverse the rules to get the necessary conjugation. The examples below illustrate this:

  • (mang- verb w/ assim.) 'kuha' -> 'manguha/nanguha/nangunguha/mangunguha'
  • (man-- verb w/ assim.) 'dukot' -> 'manukot/nanukot/nanunukot/manunukot'
  • (man-- verb w/o assim.) 'ligaw' -> 'manligaw/nanligaw/nanliligaw/manliligaw'
  • (mam-- verb w/ assim.) 'pitas' -> 'mamitas/namitas/namimitas/mamimitas'

The only thing that might trip you up is the repeated syllable. Generally, if the last letter of the prefix is next to a vowel, then the syllable to repeat made up of that last letter of the prefix + the vowel that follows. (remember that 'ng' is one letter in Filipino). Otherwise, it should be obvious what syllable to repeat. Refer to the above to see this in effect.

On to an/han- verbs:

An/Han- Verb Rules (General)
Rule Example
Root N/A tawag
Infinitive root + (h)an tawagan
Past Place in before first vowel of infinitive tinawagan
Present first two syllables of past conjugation + infinitive tinatawagan
Future infinitive with repeated first syllable tatawagan

Whether you place a 'an' or 'han' is dependent on a number of factors (irregular root, vowel placement, probably other stuff). Once again, 'an' vs 'han' will be addressed by the example given (so will any shenanigans like 'o' -> 'u' or droping the last vowel from the root), and thus, the above rules will hold for any an/han- verb (when working from a given conjugation). See below:

  • (irreg. verb) 'takip' -> 'takpan/tinakpan/tinatakpan/tatakpan
  • (irreg. verb) 'bukas' -> 'buksan/binuksan/binabuksan/bubuksan
  • (irreg. verb) 'sara' -> 'sarhan/sinarhan/sinasarhan/sasarhan
  • ('o' -> 'u' verb) 'ayos' -> 'ayusan/inayusan/inaayusan/aayusan
  • ('o' -> 'u' verb) 'tulog' -> 'tulungan/tinulungan/tinutulungan/tutulungan

The rules for i- verbs are super duper similar to the rules for an/han- verbs, you just have to account for the added 'i' (which changes up some of the counting). Hopefully the similarity is easily recognizable:

I- Verb Rules
Rule Example
Root N/A sulat
Infinitive i + root isulat
Past place in before second vowel of infinitive isinulat
Present first three syllables of past conjugation + infinitive isinusulat
Future infinitive with repeated second syllable isusulat

With an/han/i/in/hin- verbs, whenever there is an 'in' inserted into a word (which occurs in the past and present conjugations) whose root began with an 'l', it is acceptable to switch the first 'l' with the first 'n'. So for 'luto', 'ilinuto/iniluto' are acceptable past tense conjugations, and 'ilinuluto/iniluluto' are acceptable present tense conjugations. Notice that the above rules for conjugation still work, regardless of whether or not the 'l'/'n' switch occurs.

Note 1: In the back of the book, for the in-form of 'luto', 'niluto' and niluluto' are the only conjugations given for the past and present. I do not know whether or not that means 'linuto' and 'linuluto' are incorrect.

Note 2: Also from the back of the book, the root word 'prito' varies slightly from the rules given above for i-verbs (specifically the past-tense): 'prito' -> 'iprito/ipinirito (notice that it's 'ini' and not 'in')/ipiniprito/ipriprito'. I do not know why this is and I cannot (easily) find any other examples of this. If I had to guess, it has to do with the double-consonant beginning.

Note 3: One more odd i verb. 'hugas' -> 'ihugas/inihugas/inihuhugas/ihuhugas'

By now, hopefully it's clear that as long as an example conjugation is given, the above rules can be used to get you to any other conjugation. So there is no need to worry about assimilation, glottal stops, figuring out whether or not there's a dash, or whatever randomness that's ever-present in Filipino (at least with those verb-infixes).

However, a similarly 'sturdy' set of rules (centered around a given conjugation) cannot be created for in/hin- verbs. This is because of: (a) the existence of irregular in/hin- verbs (like with an/han- verbs), and (b) not all in/hin- conjugations can be arrived at from the infinitive/other conjugations (unlike with an/han- verbs).

Uh, it's easier to see when you take a look at the general in/hin- verb rules:

In/Hin- Verb Rules (General)
Rule Example
Root N/A gising
Infinitive (modified) root + (h)in gisingin
Past Place in before first vowel of unmodified root ginisng
Present first two syllables of past conjugation + unmodified root ginigising
Future infinitive with repeated first syllable gigisingin

So, unlike with an/han- verbs, in/hin- verbs do not remain consistently modified across all conjugations.

Take the root 'ayos for example: (in/hin-) 'ayusin/inayos/inaayos/aayusin' vs. (an/han-) 'ayusan/inayusan/inaayusan/aayusan. Note how the 'o/u changes. Additionally, you cannot necessarily tell if an 'in' is required or if a 'hin' is required - even given an example (e.g. 'inayos' does not tell you if it's 'ayusin or 'ayushin').

Anyway, what all that means is that you have to learn when an in/hin- root needs to be modified, which roots need an 'in' vs a 'hin', and which roots are irregular. Regarding the first two things:

  • The most common modification occurs when the last vowel of a root is an 'o': If it is, then the 'o' turns to a 'u' for the infinitive and future conjugations.
  • More or less, if the root ends in a consant, use 'in'; if it ends with a vowel, use 'hin'. Like with anything else, there are exceptions (that I unfortunately do not know).

As for irregular verbs, our kind professor has given us a list of irregular verbs for just that purpose, so study up.

  • Past: ka- word (kahapon, kanina, kamakalawa), noong phrases (noong Sabado, noong Enero, noong umaga), the word 'noon' itself.
  • Present: 'tuwing', 'ngayon' and ngayon phrases (ngayong umaga, ngayon tanghali), stuff that repeats (gabi-gabi, araw-araw, linggu-linggo)
  • Future: 'bukas', 'mamaya', mamaya phrases (mamayang gabi, mamayang hapon), 'balang araw' (and presumably 'balang linggo' or 'balang buwan'), sa- words/phrases (samakalawa, sa Linggo, sa Enero)
